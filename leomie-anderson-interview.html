Leomie Anderson is empowering women and challenging the fashion industry from within

Publish date: 2024-08-02

“I am very much on team post-pandemic activewear chic for life,” laughs model and entrepreneur Leomie Anderson, who is relishing the athleisure boom which has taken place during lockdown. The business owner, who launched her own fashion brand, Lapp – specialising in active and loungewear – four years ago, believes that we've turned a corner in terms of dress codes and have proven that we don’t need to be following a strict set of rules and restrictions that are rooted in the past.

“I feel that society is now realising that you don’t need to be wearing a suit to look professional; you don’t need to have on this protective armour for people to take you seriously,” she tells me. “I can run a whole business via Zoom and Whatsapp, so why do I need to dress a certain way to do that? Why do I need to dress in something that doesn’t make me feel like myself? I don’t want to go back to putting on a shirt or a tight, uncomfortable dress just to be taken seriously. That doesn’t make you professional; it shouldn’t be about that, it should be about what you have to say.”

Leomie Anderson with Jourdan Dunn

This is a huge part of the reason that Anderson launched Lapp. Inspired by her own experience, where she felt she could get the most done and be the best version of herself wearing something comfortable, she decided that she wanted to encourage others to discover the same.

I can run a business via Zoom and Whatsapp, so why do I need to dress a certain way to do that?

“Lapp is all about empowering female voices – it's about making women feel strong and that their voice should be heard,” she explains. “Lapp is about making women feel like the best version of themselves.”

This doesn’t come across just with the retail side of the business, where Anderson favours bold colours and designs which accentuate the female form – “Every piece is about highlighting and celebrating the female body, whatever shape you are” – but also in the content Lapp produces, including an online magazine, which focuses on female issues.

“With the blog, it's not about money – it doesn’t make any money. It is not about that. People often think that when you have a brand, you should be completely focused on the financial aspect, but to me, it’s about these moments when a young girl reaches out to me and tells me that the platform has really spoken to her – that is what makes me feel really proud.”

Every piece is about highlighting and celebrating the female body, whatever shape you are

Anderson says that she wanted the clothing label to represent and project this same feeling – and one moment early on sent that message across better than she could ever have imagined.

“A monumental point for me was when Rihanna wore Lapp to the women’s march. It was not just because it was Rihanna, but it was also the specific moment that she chose to wear it for. It was really huge and iconic for the brand and it just completely embodied the message of what I was trying to do. It put Lapp on the map as a brand for women, by women – and that is exactly what I wanted the brand to be about from the beginning.”

Courtesy of Flannels

Leomie Anderson with Munroe Berdof

Not only did this help to gain the best possible type of exposure for Lapp, but it also gave Anderson a little bit more confidence: “It really helped to shape the brand early on. It pushed me to keep going with it.”

I think I have encountered every single obstacle possible when it comes to running a business

Despite these brilliant moments though, running a business is far from easy, which Anderson is quick to admit. When I ask what some of the biggest lessons she’s learnt along the way are, she replies: “Have you got all day?”

“I think I have encountered every single obstacle possible when it comes to running a business. It is just me and two freelancers, so I have to do pretty much every aspect of it. In the early stages, it’s very easy to just want to do the fun stuff – you want to do the design, you want to do the shoots, you want to hear people tell you how amazing the pieces are, but the bottom line is that even if you do all of that right, if the business side of things is not handled and managed properly, then it doesn’t matter how good your designs are.”

“At the same time, the business side of things is not something that you can just hand over to someone else,” she adds. “You really have to build a lot of trust to do this and not everyone has the best intentions. That is something that I learnt the hard way really early on – and I am still learning to this day. You have to be careful who you trust because no one is going to fight harder for your brand than you.”

That being said, Anderson’s latest project came about because she found a group of people who understood her message and were willing to go the extra mile to make it work.

“This partnership came at a time when I was really unsure who to trust and who had genuine interest in the brand doing well, but once I sat around the table with these women and saw how much they believed in the vision and believed in the brand, it really just made me want to work with them and make this the best capsule ever.”

Courtesy of Flannels

Leomie Anderson with Neelam Gill

The collaboration is a 23-piece collection with luxury retailer Flannels, which launches this week in celebration of International Women’s Day and, like all of Lapp’s collections, is made up of seamless sportswear in a wide range of sizes, helping to make activewear more inclusive. The two brands have worked closely together for over six months to develop and produce the collection, which saw Flannels keen to embrace Anderson’s passion for social activism and feminism.

“The label powerfully showcases where the foundations of the brand stemmed from, raising awareness for the need to diversify the fashion landscape across numerous avenues, through the shapes, sizes and styles they are offering,” Emma iLori, Flannels’ head of womenswear elevation says.

Courtesy of Flannels

Leomie Anderson with Jourdan Dunn

Anderson is not only working to empower women’s voices, but is also fighting for more diversity in the industry, something which she feels has only been dealt with on a level that is “very surface” so far, despite big claims made by brands last summer.

I am usually the only Black person on set

“Look, doing a campaign with more racial diversity is great – and when I was growing up, I wish there had been more representation in fashion imagery, so of course I am happy about that, but I am still usually the only Black person on set. If they tell me that the CEO is going to stop by the shoot, I know that is not going to be a Black person. For me now, if businesses want to make real progress and tangible change, they need to look within and see how the structure of their company may be upholding the oppression of Black people and systemic racism.

“It’s all good putting me as a Black girl as the face of your brand, but how many people at your company are Black? How many credits are there going to be for a Black photographer, a Black make-up artist or a Black stylist?

“This is a very big part of the problem. As long as we stay satisfied with them putting a Black girl in the campaign, then nothing is ever going to change.”

Lapp's capsule collection is available to shop on Flannels now.
