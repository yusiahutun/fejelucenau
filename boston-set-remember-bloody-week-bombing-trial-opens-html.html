Boston marathon survivor Karen McWatters tells court about friend's last moments

Publish date: 2024-02-26

A woman who lost her leg in the Boston bombing told the court on Wednesday how she watched her friend utter her last words before she died from horrific blast injuries.

Karen McWatters went to the 2013 marathon with Krystle Campbell to watch her boyfriend run the race, she testified on the first day of trial on Wednesday. The pair were near the finish line so they could photograph McWatters' boyfriend when the bombs went off. 

Mrs McWatters said she remembered 'screaming and smoke' and wondered why she was lying on the ground. She looked at her leg and realized 'something terrible had happened to us'.

The survivor kept her composure as she testified that although her foot was turned the wrong way and the ground so hot, she made it to Krystle and pressed their faces together.

Mrs McWatters said: 'I didn't know how bad she was hurt. She said her legs hurt, then her hand went limp in mine and she never spoke again.' 

The Boston court, which was packed with victims' families and survivors, was shown a surveillance picture of the two friends. Amid a horrific scene, Campbell's legs had been blown off.

Bomber Dzhokhar Tsarnaev, 21, watched the video screen and stared at the table in the courtroom on Wednesday but did not look at the witnesses on the stand.

He faces the death penalty if convicted. He has pleaded not guilty to the 30 counts against him which also include shooting dead a Boston police officer, Sean Collier.

In her testimony, Mrs McWatters described being scared and screaming going on all around. She said the smell in the air was so unusual that she thought it must be a bomb.

The two friends were separated and taken to separate hospitals. Mrs McWatters, who married last year, said she feared at that point that her friend would die. Mrs McWatters' leg was amputated two days after the April 15 bombing.

The jury was also shown a 'touristy photo' that the two women had taken together before the marathon.  

The bombing killed restaurant manager Miss Campbell, 29; Chinese Boston University graduate student Lingzi Lu, 23 and eight-year-old Martin Richard. 

Tsarnaev killed three people and injured 264 with a pair of homemade pressure-cooker bombs at the Boston Marathon. His alleged accomplice, brother Tamerlan, died in a shoot-out with police four days later. 

The court also heard from other maimed female survivors of the Boston bombing, who wept on the stand as they told the court how they believed they would die from their horrific injuries.

Rebekah Gregory, 27, from Houston, Texas, who lost half of her left leg after 18 surgeries, testified.

Ms Gregory was at the marathon to cheer on family with her five-year-old son Noah. She told the court that the last thing she remembered was entertaining Noah who was bored watching the runners.

She said that the force of the blast threw her backwards and immediately she began looking for her son - but realized she had bones sticking out of her legs and arms and flesh hanging from her body. 

She told the court that she could hear Noah screaming 'mommy' and asked God to take her but not her son.

Ms Gregory began to weep as she said that as she scanned for Noah, she saw the body of Krystle Campbell lying behind her.

She recalled that an emergency responder told her she was going to be OK then yelled: 'We have an amputee!' 

Several jurors were close to tears as Ms Gregory told the court that she still has shrapnel in her body and each time a piece works its way to the surface, she will have an operation. 

Her son Noah suffered a shrapnel injury to his leg and head. 

Ms Gregory was shown a photo in court in which she was able to identify herself and her family with bomber, Tamerlan Tsarnaev, standing directly behind.

The next photo showed her family thrown across the sidewalk covered in blood and torn clothing.

Sydney Corcoran also took the stand on Wednesday afternoon. She went to the Boston Marathon two years ago with her mother Celeste to watch her aunt run the race.

Mrs Corcoran, who lost her legs in the bombing, watched her daughter from the front row of the public gallery. 

Miss Corcoran recalled being enveloped in smoke and finding herself lying on the ground surrounded by people tending her injuries.

'I remember thinking that, this was it. I'm going to die. I'm not going to make it,' said the college sophomore, who lost a large amount of blood from shrapnel wounds to her right leg.

'I remember having moments of panic when I would look around and I would see carnage and just blood on the street,' she testified. 'I had no idea what had happened, I just knew it was bad.' 

'WHERE IS THE BLOOD COMING FROM?' FIRST BOMBING WITNESSES CALLED TO TESTIFY AT TRIAL

A bystander testified how he could smell burning flesh and saw severed limbs following the Boston bombings as screaming victims lay writhing in pain, drenched in pools of blood.

Colton Kilgore, from Ashville, North Carolina, took the stand on Wednesday afternoon on the first day of bomber Dzhokhar Tsarnaev's trial. 

Mr Kilgore had been videotaping the Boston Marathon, as his mother-in-law was competing, when the first blast ripped through the crowd at the finish line on April 15, 2013.

The court, which included victims' families and maimed survivors, watched Mr Kilgore's horrifying video which was left rolling in the aftermath of the terror attack.

On the video, victims could be seen lying amid shards of burning metal and ball bearings. Mr Kilgore said that he could smell acrid smoke, blood and flesh. A severed leg of one victim is seen on the ground.

As Mr Kilgore sets down the camera and rushes to help the wounded, a blonde woman is seen on screen writhing in pain. 

Pools of blood cover the sidewalk and the audio captures crying and the screaming of a woman with a hole blown in her lower leg. A first responder can be heard wondering aloud: 'Where is this blood coming from?' 

Prosecution witness Shane O'Hara, manager of the Marathon Sports, testified on Wednesday about people taking clothing to staunch victims' bleeding

A first responder was seen sliding in a pool of blood.

Mr Kilgore told the court that he was thrown by the force of the blast which left him dizzy and with hearing loss.

He was able to use his belt as a tourniquet in the bombing aftermath. 

His five-year-old nephew Noah, Rebekah Gregory's son, suffered a shrapnel wound to the leg. On the footage, Mr Kilgore can be heard reassuring Noah and Rebekah that they are going to be OK.

The trial also heard from a store manager who described how bystanders emptied shelves of clothing to make tourniquets for bleeding victims.

Shane O'Hara runs Marathon Sports on Boylston Street close to where the first bomb exploded on April 15, 2013. 

Mr O'Hara told prosecutors that he was working the day of the marathon and that the store was typically busy. 

The court was shown a surveillance camera still of Mr O'Hara in the doorway, watching the race.

He said he remembered the day very well and fought back tears adding that he thinks about the bombing 'every day'. 

He described the sound of the explosion as 'like a big cannon' and said the store window shattered and was engulfed in smoke.

The jury was shown surveillance video from inside the sports store. Following the blast, people were seen running in and grabbing clothing from the shelves to staunch people's bleeding.  

Mr O'Hara followed the first witness, head of the Boston Athletic Association, Thomas Grilk.

Mr Grilk told the court that the annual Boston Marathon is an 'aspiration' for thousands of runners and a great commitment for the volunteers who make it possible. 

He also spoke about the security measures put in place.

The jury was then shown a video of the explosions close to the marathon finish line. Mr Grilk said that following the bombs, 5,600 runners were turned back from the finish line. 

He also described the emergency responders response and that of the BAA volunteers.

'Those people did a magnificent job,' he told the court.

'HE PRETENDED TO BE A SPECTATOR BUT HE HAD MURDER IN HIS HEART': U.S. ATTORNEY'S OPENING STATEMENT

Earlier on Wednesday, U.S. Attorney William Weinreb said in his opening statement that Tsarnaev had purposely placed a bomb behind a row of children which included eight-year-old Martin Richard, who was killed in the attack.

'His [Dzhokhar] goal that day was to kill and maim as many people as possible,' Weinreb said. 'He pretended to be a spectator but had murder in his heart.'

Martin's parents, Bill and Denise Richard, wept but listened stoically as Weinreb said: 'The defendant blew large holes into Martin and he lost his arms'. The child lost so much blood there was almost none left in his body, the jury heard.

Mr Richard, who had left the courtroom at the beginning of proceedings, returned at the mention of his son. 

Weinreb said following the bomb blast, Mrs Richard was unable to see her dying son because she had been blinded in one eye by shrapnel.

The bombing killed restaurant manager Krystle Campbell, 29 and Chinese Boston University graduate student Lingzi Lu, 23. 

MIT police officer Sean Collier, 27, was fatally shot three days later by the bombers. The prosecution showed jurors the photos of each victim during opening statements at the Boston federal courthouse. 

Weinreb described the violent deaths of Miss Campbell and Miss Lu, saying the latter bled to death on the sidewalk.

He then told the jury that while people lay dying on the streets 'the defendant ran away'.

But the lawyer also told the court: 'In the midst of the chaos, some people sprang into action. There were a lot of heroes that day, and you'll hear from some.'

The packed courtroom had fallen silent as the 21-year-old defendant entered, dressed in a black suit and white shirt with his beard trimmed into a goatee.

He kept his head down and did not look at those in the public gallery. Some of those in court stared at his back while others turned their heads away. 

Tsarnaev at times listened intently and then appeared to lose interest, CBS reported, during the opening statements. 

U.S. attorney Weinreb then told the jury that officials had found shreds of the backpack, wires and bomb parts all over Boylston Street. Doctors found the same inside victims.

Around 20 minutes after the devastation, Tsarnaev was seen on surveillance camera entering a Whole Foods nearby and buying milk.

The victims: From left Krystle Campbell, 29; Lu Lingzi, a graduate student from China; and eight-year-old Martin Richard were all killed in the bombings near the finish line of the Boston Marathon on April 15, 2013

Police officer Sean Collier was shot dead by the Boston bombers on April 18, 2013

He then returned to his apartment at University of Massachusetts Dartmouth and partied, went to the gym and played video games.

He replied to one friend's text: 'Better not text me, my friend, LOL', the court heard on Wednesday. 

Tsarnaev had become a terrorist as a protest to U.S. foreign policy and 'believed what he had done was good, that he is a soldier in a holy war against America and had taken a step to reaching paradise'.

The prosecution immediately sought to tear down the image of Dzhokhar Tsarnaev as the disciple of his accomplice older brother, Tamerlan. 

U.S. Attorney Weinreb said the younger brother's computer was loaded with anti-American propaganda and images glorifying terrorism.

He also said there were instructions on how to make pressure-cooker bombs and where to place them in a crowd.

The two brothers had prepped for their attack together for many months, the jury heard, going to practise shooting handguns at a New Hampshire firing range. 

Four days after the bombing, as police closed in on the brothers in Watertown, Massachusetts, the suspects shot Officer Sean Collier 'between the eyes' the prosecution said. 

When his colleague, Officer Dic Donohue responded, he was shot in the leg and lost so much blood he almost died. Officer Donohue listened in court on Wednesday from the public gallery.

As police engaged the brothers in a shootout, Weinreb said that Dzhokhar 'cared more about killing them than he cared about his brother... he ran right over his brother and dragged him.'

The prosecution concluded: 'We will ask you to find Tsarnaev guilty of all 30 counts.'

Throughout the statement, the accused bomber stared straight ahead, CBS reported. He could be sentenced to death if convicted of charges.

DEFENSE HOLD TSARNAEV RESPONSIBLE FOR THE BOMBINGS.... BUT SAY HE SHOULD NOT BE EXECUTED AS BROTHER WAS IN CHARGE

Defense lawyer Judy Clarke then rose to give the opening statement for her side. She began: 'We meet in the most tragic of circumstances.'

She did not deny the atrocities that the Tsarnaev brothers inflicted on Boston two years ago and said there was no question that Dzhokhar had left a bomb on Boylston Street.

The defense will not 'side-step' his responsibilities for his actions but were focused on the sentencing portion of the trial, the jury heard.

Ms Clarke said: 'We must hold [Tsarnaev] responsible for the horrible things he did... but he took that path because of his brother's influence.'

She added: 'Tamerlan didn't put a gun to Tsarnaev's head, but he had huge influence over his brother.' 

BOSTON COURTROOM PACKED WITH VICTIMS' FAMILIES AND MAIMED SURVIVORS

The courtroom was packed with family members of the victims on Wednesday, as well as some of those who were wounded, 16 of whom lost legs. 

Heather Abbott, who had her leg amputated after the terror attack, was pictured being helped off a bus outside federal court to attend the trial. 

Marc Fucarile, who lost his right leg in the bomb blast, was also pictured leaving court with his wife during lunchtime recess on Wednesday. 

Other survivors in the courtroom included Officer Dic Donohue who was seriously wounded after being shot in Watertown when his friend, Officer Collier, was killed. 

Watertown's police chief, Ed Deveraux, was also in the public gallery.  

Carlos Arredondo and wife were also in court, CBS reported. Mr Arredondo helped in the aftermath of the bombing and became part of one of the most iconic images of the atrocity as he rushed Jeff Bauman, who lost both his legs, to an ambulance in a wheelchair.  

COURT BUILDING ON LOCKDOWN AS TERRORIST TRIAL BEGINS

Wednesday's proceedings began with U.S. District Judge George O'Toole talking to the defense team and prosecutors. 

He denied the defense's fourth attempt to have the trial moved out of Boston after lawyers claimed Tsarnaev cannot be fairly judged because too many people are personally connected to the case.

However the judge said that he would allowed the defense to talk to the jury about Tsarnaev's background, including details of his childhood and relationship with older brother, Tamerlan.

The jury was brought in and the judge instructed them on their role in the trial, emphasizing they must not discuss case among themselves or with others. .

Prosecutors told the judge on Monday that they may want to show as evidence autopsy photos of the attack's victims.

They also want to play clips from an FBI news conference where officials released photos of Tsarnaev and his older brother identifying them as suspects and setting off a course of events that led to a day-long lock-down of most of the Boston area amid a massive manhunt. 

Defense attorneys, meanwhile, aim to portray Tsarnaev as having been under the spell of his 26-year-old brother, Tamerlan, who they contend was the mastermind behind the attack. 

Tamerlan died following an April 18, 2013, gun battle with police.

Tsarnaev's lawyers fought right up until the last minute to have the trial moved outside of Massachusetts, arguing that the emotional impact of the bombings ran too deep in the state and too many people had personal connections to the case.

Their requests were rejected by Judge George O'Toole Jr. and the 1st U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals.  

The trial is expected to be one of the most closely-watched terror cases in years.

A replaying of some of the graphic evidence of the attack could trigger fears among people who lived in the area during the bombing week, said Alice LoCicero, a Boston Medical Center psychologist who has researched the psychology of terrorist attacks.

'What people might anticipate is a return of some of the same feelings that they had at the time of the initial bombing,' LoCicero said. 'A lot of fear may be associated with reliving some of those events.'

A panel of 10 women and eight men, all white, were chosen to hear Tsarnaev's trial and, if they find him guilty, to determine whether to sentence him to death or life in prison without the possibility of parole in proceedings expected to last into June. 

Security was tight around Boston's waterfront federal courthouse, where officials have closed some nearby roads amid several large-scale construction projects.

Defense attorneys had cited the 'Boston Strong' signs that hung on some of those sites in their four requests to move the trial out of the city that was the site of the largest mass-casualty attack on U.S. soil since September 11, 2001.
