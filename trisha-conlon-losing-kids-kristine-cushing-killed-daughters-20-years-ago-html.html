I'm losing my kids to a killer: Mother stunned to find her sons will live with woman who killed her own daughters 20 years ago

Maternal instinct: Trisha Conlon doesn't feel comfortable with her teenage boys living with a woman who shot and killed her own daughters in their sleep 20 years ago

Trisha Conlon's motherly instinct seems reasonable: She doesn't want her teen boys living with a woman who killed her own daughters 20 years ago.

But that will happen on Sunday thanks to a bizarre custody dispute with her ex-husband, retired Marine fighter pilot Lt. Col. John P. Cushing Jr.

Cushing, who lives on Vashon Island south of Seattle, is back with his first wife, Kristine, who used a .38-caliber handgun in the deaths of their four and eight-year-old girls as they slept in their California home in 1991.

The killings shocked the well-to-do community of Laguna Niguel.

Many wondered how Kristine Cushing - a ‘super-mom’ ferrying her girls to music, soccer, dance and the orthodontist as her husband was on military assignments overseas - could snap so tragically.

She blamed the killings on a bad reaction to the antidepressant Prozac, and was found not guilty by reason of insanity

After four years in a mental institution and a decade of psychiatric monitoring, Kristine Cushing received an unconditional release.

California authorities determined she posed no risk.

But that's done little to soothe Conlon, who went to court to change the parenting plan for the two sons she had with Cushing after learning Kristine had returned to his home.

Ms Conlon said: ‘I just don't understand how a person could have marital relations with the person who killed their children.

'It just doesn't make sense to me.’

Kristine Cushing blamed the killing of her daughters on a bad reaction to the antidepressant Prozac, and was found not guilty by reason of insanity

However, the court ruled against her.

Commissioner Leonid Ponomarchuk said that since the boys had been spending time with Kristine Cushing since 2008 with no problems — even if it was unknown to Conlon — there was no evidence of a change in situation that would warrant an alteration of the parenting plan.

Mr Ponomarchuk said: ‘I have to look at this dispassionately. Would I ever want my children around her? I would say no. But that is an emotional reaction coming from a parent.’

In court declarations, Mr Cushing has emphasized that Kristine was considered temporarily insane - and thus, ‘There was no crime committed.’

He called the incident 'a horrible tragedy that resulted in the deaths of our two daughters.

'[Conlon] and her counsel seem to feel that anyone who suffers from temporary insanity is incapable of recovering from that condition.'

He added: ‘Kristine M. Cushing is doing well. She is busy, enjoys life and loves me and my sons.’

Cushing noted that he stores his personal guns at a friend's home when he's not using them at a gun range.

Kristine Cushing said in her own declaration, ‘I love Samuel and Stephen very much and have a good relationship with both of them.’

Conlon lives in Silverton, Oregon. She and Cushing were married in 1995 and divorced in 2004.

Their older son, 14-year-old Stephen, lives with her during the school year, while 13-year-old Sam lives with Cushing.

The boys are together during holidays and vacations, which they split between their parents.

Conlon suspected Kristine, who remarried Mr Cushing in 2005, might have been back in Cushing's life earlier, but said she didn't learn it for sure until 2007, when she received a call from a Washington state Child Protective Services worker.

The worker told her that CPS had just received a call from Kristine's therapist, who reported that Kristine was living with children again.

Conlon threatened then to go to court to seek a change in the boys' parenting plan, court records show.

Instead, Mr Cushing told her not to worry about it: Kristine had decided to divorce him and move out.

But the divorce was never finalized, and Kristine moved back in.

According to a court declaration by Mr Cushing, that was in March 2008.

For the next three years, Ms Conlon said, she had no idea her boys were spending time with Kristine Cushing.

Mrs Cushing deliberately concealed that fact, Ms Conlon claimed, by telling the boys to refer to their stepmom by a different name — ‘Mrs. M.’

Conlon said that early this year she became suspicious again that Kristine was back in the house.

She arrived at Cushing's home to drop off Stephen for spring break and saw a painting Kristine had made hanging on a wall. It was dated 2010.

She and her attorney, Todd DeVallance, hired a private investigator, who confirmed it.

On Monday, Conlon went to court in mid-June and was granted full custody of the boys for 30 days, at which time she would have to make a showing in court that the parenting plan should be permanently modified.

Mr DeVallance was outraged at Ponomarchuk's decision to deny.

He said: There's not a parent I've talked to who've said they'd let their children in a home with a woman who has executed her children.’

‘It's absurd to say, “Well, she hasn't killed anybody recently.” This isn't right.'

Neither Mr Cushing nor his attorney returned calls seeking comment.

Mr DeVallance and Conlon are appealing the decision to the King County Superior Court judge assigned to their case, but cannot get a hearing until Aug. 25.

In the meantime, Conlon will drop the boys off with Cushing on Sunday, she said.
